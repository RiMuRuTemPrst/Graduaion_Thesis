\subsection{Problem Statement}

\subsubsection*{The Image Data Explosion}

The digital world faces an unprecedented explosion in visual data generation. Modern applications across surveillance, medical imaging, remote sensing, and social media platforms continuously produce massive volumes of high-resolution visual content. In 2025 alone, the global data sphere reached 181 zettabytes, with videos accounting for approximately 82\% of all internet traffic \cite{explodingtopics2025}. This exponential growth creates a critical mismatch with existing storage and processing infrastructure:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rising Storage Costs:} Hard disk and solid-state drivers struggle to keep pace with petabyte-scale data generation. Storage infrastructure has become a significant bottleneck and cost driver for data-intensive applications.

    \item \textbf{Edge Processing Demand:} Real-time, high-resolution image compression on embedded IoT and edge devices is essential for surveillance systems, autonomous vehicles, medical devices, and remote monitoring applications. These systems must operate with strict latency, power, and memory constraints.

    \item \textbf{Legacy Compression Limitations:} Traditional standards such as JPEG and JPEG2000 rely on handcrafted transforms and fixed pipelines. They exhibit visible artifacts at high compression ratios and fail to adapt to modern image statistics, limiting their effectiveness for contemporary applications.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{The Case for Learned Image Compression}

Learned image compression based on deep learning has emerged as a promising solution to address these challenges. Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)-based models, particularly the High-Fidelity Image Compression (HiFiC) framework, have demonstrated state-of-the-art perceptual quality at low bitrates by leveraging adversarial training to generate visually pleasing reconstructions \cite{mentzer2020high}. Unlike traditional schemes, HiFiC preserves fine-grained textures and semantic details by incorporating a generator--discriminator architecture, offering superior rate--distortion performance and adaptability to diverse image content.

However, the deployment of GAN-based compression models in real-world systems faces a critical challenge: substantial computational complexity. The HiFiC inference pipeline consists of deep convolutional networks with multiple residual blocks, high-dimensional feature maps, and intensive arithmetic operations. This makes direct deployment on general-purpose processors (CPUs, GPUs) inefficient for real-time or resource-constrained embedded applications, creating a gap between algorithmic superiority and practical deployability.

\subsubsection*{Hardware Acceleration on FPGA}

Field-Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs) provide an attractive platform for addressing this deployment gap. They offer inherent parallelism, fine-grained reconfigurability, and favorable performance--power trade-offs compared to general-purpose processors. Modern heterogeneous FPGA systems enable tight integration between programmable logic and embedded processors (ARM Cortex), facilitating a hardware--software co-design paradigm where computationally intensive kernels are offloaded to dedicated accelerators while control logic and less demanding tasks execute in software.

\subsubsection*{Project Motivation}

In this work, we present a hardware--software co-design approach for learned image compression based on a quantized HiFiC GAN model targeting embedded deployment. The proposed design accelerates the HiFiC generator pipeline on FPGA using High-Level Synthesis (HLS), with quantization to FP16 mixed-precision to reduce hardware resource requirements while maintaining acceptable numerical accuracy. The resulting system demonstrates how algorithmic innovation (GAN-based compression) and hardware optimization (FPGA acceleration) can be combined to enable practical, real-time image compression on resource-constrained platforms.

\subsection{Review of Previous Works}

Learned image compression has been an active research area, with GAN-based models such as HiFiC achieving state-of-the-art perceptual quality at low bitrates \cite{mentzer2020high}. On the hardware side, a large body of work has accelerated convolutional neural networks on FPGA by exploiting parallel processing elements, on-chip buffering, and customized dataflow to improve throughput and energy efficiency. However, these efforts have mostly targeted classification or object-detection networks rather than generative image-compression models.

Table~\ref{tab:model_survey} surveys representative learned image compression models together with the traditional VVC/H.266 video coding standard. These span traditional coding \cite{bross2021vvc}, CNN-based variational autoencoders \cite{cheng2020learned}, transformer-based models \cite{zou2022stf}, hybrid CNN--transformer designs \cite{liu2023tcm}, and GAN-based perceptual compression \cite{mentzer2020high}. The models optimized purely for rate--distortion (Cheng~2020, STF, TCM) reach higher PSNR and MS-SSIM, whereas HiFiC, which is optimized for perceptual quality, attains the best (lowest) LPIPS at the cost of a lower PSNR. Because perceptual quality at low bitrates is the priority for the target applications, and because the HiFiC generator is built from repeated residual blocks that form a regular, hardware-friendly computational pattern, HiFiC is selected as the model to accelerate in this thesis.

To the best of our knowledge, the HiFiC GAN model has not previously been implemented on an FPGA. Existing learned-compression accelerators do not address the specific computational pattern of the HiFiC generator, which is dominated by repeated residual blocks containing convolution, normalization, activation, and skip connections. This thesis therefore addresses that gap by designing a hardware--software co-design that accelerates the HiFiC generator ResBlock on FPGA. Because no prior FPGA implementation of the same model exists for a direct head-to-head comparison, the proposed design is instead evaluated against the software (CPU) execution baseline, as presented in Chapter~4.

\subsection{Research Objectives}

The main objective of this thesis is to design and implement an FPGA-based accelerator for the quantized HiFiC GAN inference pipeline using a hardware--software co-design methodology. The specific contributions of the project are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A hardware--software co-design framework for HiFiC GAN inference targeting learned image compression applications.
    \item An FPGA-based accelerator architecture optimized for quantized GAN workloads using streaming dataflow and parallel processing elements.
    \item A practical implementation and evaluation on the ZCU104 FPGA platform, demonstrating feasible performance, resource efficiency, and numerical accuracy.
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:design_spec} gives an overview of the design. The HiFiC generator transforms the hyper-latent representation into the reconstructed $256\times256\times3$ RGB image through a convolution input block, the residual blocks, the up-convolution blocks, and a final output convolution. \subsubsection{Functional Requirements}

The proposed system must satisfy the following functional requirements:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accelerator Input and Output:} Take the hyper-latent representation ($16 \times 16 \times 960$) produced by the compression front-end as input and generate the reconstructed RGB image ($256 \times 256 \times 3$) as output.
    \item \textbf{Accelerator Kernel:} Implement a hardware accelerator for the complete HiFiC generator (decoder), composed of the Fusion Core (initial convolution block, nine residual blocks, and the global skip addition), the UpConv Block Core (four transposed-convolution up-sampling blocks), and the Conv77 Core (final $7\times7$ output convolution).
    \item \textbf{Model Support:} Support the quantized HiFiC GAN inference pipeline with mixed-precision arithmetic (FP16 for computations, FP32 for reference baseline).
    \item \textbf{Hardware--Software Interface:} Implement PS--PL communication through AXI4-Lite (control signals) and AXI Master (data movement) to enable seamless integration with the ARM Cortex-A53 processor on the ZCU104 platform.
    \item \textbf{Functional Correctness:} Guarantee that hardware-accelerated compression produces numerically equivalent results compared to the software (CPU) reference implementation within acceptable tolerance bounds.
    \item \textbf{Compression Pipeline:} Support the complete image compression and reconstruction flow, from input image to final decompressed output with maintained visual quality.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implementation Plan and Timeline}

To meet the design specifications and requirements defined above, this project adopts a set of hardware-oriented methodologies, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:methodologies}. These methodologies follow directly from the streaming, convolution-heavy nature of the HiFiC generator and from the resource constraints of the ZCU104 platform.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Proposed methodologies used in the project}
\label{tab:methodologies}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{|c|p{0.34\textwidth}|X|}
\hline
\textbf{No.} & \textbf{Methodology} & \textbf{Impact} \\
\hline
1 & Hardware--software co-design partitioning & Keeps the irregular encoder, hyperprior, and entropy stages in software and offloads the compute-bound generator to the FPGA, removing the dominant runtime bottleneck \\
\hline
2 & High-Level Synthesis (Vitis HLS) design flow & Generates and iterates RTL from a C/C++ reference, shortening the design cycle \\
\hline
3 & Mixed-precision FP16 quantization & Reduces memory footprint and on-chip resource usage while preserving reconstruction quality \\
\hline
4 & Streaming line-buffer and sliding-window convolution & Converts repeated external-memory traffic into on-chip data reuse for high-throughput convolution \\
\hline
5 & Two-pass convolution--normalization with a rotating accumulator & Overlaps the convolution and normalization passes and sustains an initiation interval of one, maximizing MAC utilization \\
\hline
6 & Single shared, reusable compute engine per core & Reuses one hardware instance across the repeated residual blocks and stages, fitting the design within the tight on-chip resource budget \\
\hline
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}
